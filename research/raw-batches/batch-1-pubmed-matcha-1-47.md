## HIGH PRIORITY — new compound/quantitative data

1. **Baba, Takihara, Okamura (2024).** "Matcha Does Not Affect Electroencephalography during Sleep but May Enhance Mental Well-Being: A Randomized Placebo-Controlled Clinical Trial." *Nutrients*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39275223/
   - The daily study dose was **2.7 g matcha** containing **50.3 mg theanine, 301.4 mg catechins, 71.5 mg caffeine**. Normalized per gram of matcha powder: ≈18.6 mg/g theanine, ≈111.6 mg/g total catechins, ≈26.5 mg/g caffeine. This is a directly-reported, real-product composition breakdown (not a cultivar/grade study per se, but a usable "commercial matcha" composition data point) — the catechins figure in particular is much higher than the current DB's Ceremonial/Culinary Goto et al. EGCG-only numbers (59.3/75.1 mg/g), though it's *total catechins* not EGCG alone, so not directly comparable without decomposition.

2. **Nakao et al. (2024).** "Multimodal inhibitory effect of matcha on [pathogen, *Porphyromonas gingivalis*]." *Microbiology Spectrum*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38771061/
   - Catechin profile breakdown of the matcha extract used: **EGCG = 57.3% of total matcha catechins** (MIC 500 µM), **EGC = 18.5% of total catechins** (MIC 250 µM), and **pyrogallol-type catechins (EGCG+EGC+ECG-type) = 61.7–76.9% of total catechins**. Useful as a relative catechin-profile corroboration (EGCG as dominant catechin) even though it's percentage-of-total rather than absolute mg/g.

3. **Kika et al. (2024).** "Matcha Green Tea: Chemical Composition, Phenolic Acids, Caffeine and Fatty Acid Profile." *Foods*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38672839/
   - Direct compositional analysis: **caffeine = 2213.492 µg/g** (≈2.21 mg/g — flag: this is notably *lower* than most literature values for matcha caffeine, which typically run 15–35 mg/g dry weight; worth sanity-checking the original paper's units/extraction basis before using), **gallic acid (highest phenolic acid) = 252.3755 µg/g**. Also: total dietary fiber 56.1 g/100g (insoluble 52.8, soluble 3.3), protein 17.3 g/100g, total fat 7.285 g/100g (mainly linoleic + α-linolenic acid). No cultivar/grade breakdown given in abstract.

4. **Kolackova et al. (2020).** "Matcha Tea: Analysis of Nutritional Composition, Phenolics and Antioxidant Activity." *Plant Foods for Human Nutrition*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31832980/
   - Methanol-extract phenolic values: **flavonoids 99–139 mg RE/g**, **total phenolics 169–273 mg GAE/g**, **chlorogenic acid up to 4800 µg/g**, **sinapic acid up to 1400 µg/g**, **gallic acid up to 423 µg/g**. Also macro composition: crude fiber 10.7–17.5%, neutral-detergent fiber 30.7–42.8%, protein 20.3–35.0%, organic matter digestibility 59.4–69.7%. No L-theanine/EGCG numbers given, but notes "chlorophyll b was a stronger contributor to antioxidant activity than chlorophyll a."

5. **Rusak, Sola, Vujcic Bok (2021).** "Matcha and Sencha green tea extracts with regard to their phenolics pattern and antioxidant and antidiabetic activity during in vitro digestion." *Journal of Food Science and Technology* (PMC8292538 full text). https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34366474/
   - Direct **matcha-vs-sencha** comparative data (relative multipliers, not absolute mg/g), post-gastric-digestion: total phenols **2.6×** higher in matcha, total flavonoids **1.4×**, total flavanols **1.2×**, antioxidant activity ABTS **3.2×**, FRAP **1.1×**, α-glucosidase inhibition **1.8×** higher in matcha. Post-intestinal-digestion: total phenols **3.4×**, flavanols **1.7×**, ABTS **2.4×**, FRAP **2.0×**, α-glucosidase inhibition **1.7×** higher in matcha (differences "neutralized" for some parameters after full intestinal digestion). Strong corroboration that matcha's bioavailable phenolic/antioxidant load substantially exceeds standard (sencha) green tea.

6. **Jakubczyk et al. (2020).** "Antioxidant Properties and Nutritional Composition of Matcha Green Tea." *Foods*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32290537/
   - Infusion data (1.75 g matcha / 100 mL water): **flavonoids 1968.8 mg/L**, **polyphenols 1765.1 mg/L**, **vitamin C 44.8 mg/L**, **DPPH 41.2%** (10× dilution), **FRAP 6129.5 µM Fe(II)/dm³**. No L-theanine/EGCG/caffeine numbers in the abstract itself (mentioned qualitatively only), but usable secondary composition/antioxidant data if converted to a dry-weight basis.

7. **Jakubczyk et al. (2022).** "Fluoride Content of Matcha Tea Depending on Leaf Harvest Time and Brewing Conditions." *Nutrients*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35745280/
   - New compound not currently tracked by the DB: **fluoride in brewed infusion = 3.36–4.03 mg/L** (across harvest times and 25/70/80/90°C brewing temps); **fluoride in dry matcha powder = 118.39–121.65 mg/kg** (≈0.118–0.122 mg/g). Notably, fluoride content was high and largely independent of harvest time or water temperature. Could be a candidate for a future "other compounds" field if the site ever expands beyond theanine/EGCG.

8. **Unno et al. (2018).** "Stress-Reducing Function of Matcha Green Tea in Animal Experiments and Clinical Trials." *Nutrients*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30308973/
   - Not a single number but a mechanistic/ratio finding directly relevant to grade-quality reasoning: mouse studies showed matcha's anxiolytic/stress-reducing effect held only **when the molar ratio of (caffeine + EGCG) : (theanine + arginine) was less than 2**; above that ratio the effect was lost. A 39-person human trial found matcha-fed group had significantly lower anxiety than placebo. This gives a formula for predicting which matcha compositions (i.e., higher-theanine/shaded, lower-catechin grades) would be expected to have stronger calming effects — potentially useful framing for the site's grade/cultivar compound data.

9. **Luo et al. (2023).** "Variations of main quality components of matcha from different regions in the Chinese market." *Frontiers in Nutrition*. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36969824/ (PubMed itself CAPTCHA-blocked; retrieved via Semantic Scholar abstract, which may be partial)
   - Reported the **ratio of tea polyphenols to amino acids (RTA)**: 9 of 11 sampled matchas had RTA < 10 (amino acids, i.e., theanine, are the denominator — lower RTA implies relatively higher amino-acid/theanine content). The abstract as retrieved focused mostly on volatile aroma compounds (HS-SPME 1563.59–2754.09 mg/L; SAFE 1009.21–1661.98 mg/L) rather than absolute theanine/EGCG mg/g by region — the underlying paper likely has a full regional theanine/catechin table in the body/tables that isn't visible in this abstract-only fetch. **Flag for potential follow-up: try to get full text**, since this is exactly the "quality components by region" study the DB would want.

## Health effects

RCTs / clinical and mechanistic studies relevant to a future public-facing health/citations page:

- **Kochman et al. 2020** (review) — matcha catechins/theanine/caffeine underlie broad health-benefit claims; first systematic review of matcha health benefits (Molecules).
- **Sokary et al. 2022** (critical review of human/animal studies) — matcha shows modest effects on stress reduction, attention/memory; encouraging but limited cardio-metabolic and anti-tumor evidence in animals; authors conclude evidence is "still limited" (Curr Res Food Sci).
- **Shigeta et al. 2023** (RCT, 8/12-week resistance training) — 1.5 g matcha beverage 2×/day: greater leg-strength gains, lower post-exercise fatigue and salivary cortisol, greater skeletal muscle mass gain, and gut microbiota shifts (Ruminococcus, Butyricimonas, Oscillospira) correlating with strength gains (Nutrition Journal).
- **Willems, Sahin, Cook 2018** (crossover, n=13 women) — 1 g "premium grade" matcha drinks before/during 30-min brisk walk: RER decreased (0.84→0.82, p<.01) and fat oxidation increased (0.31→0.35 g/min, p<.01); authors caution against overstating practical weight-loss relevance (Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab).
- **Ye et al. 2023** (comprehensive review) — production, food applications, GI fate of matcha phenolics; fiber-bound phenolics linked to improved bioavailability via gut microbiota modulation (Crit Rev Food Sci Nutr).
- **Uchida et al. 2024** (12-month RCT, n=99, 2 g/day matcha vs placebo, cognitive decline/MCI population) — significant improvement only in "social acuity" (facial emotion perception, p=0.028); primary MoCA/ADCS-ADL outcomes unchanged; sleep quality (PSQI) showed a non-significant improvement trend (PLoS One).
- **Sakurai et al. 2020** (12-week RCT, n=61, 3 g/day) — women in the active group showed significant MoCA improvement; inverse correlation between dietary (non-matcha) vitamin K intake and cognitive change (Nutrients).
- **Baba, Takihara, Okamura 2024** (RCT, 2.7 g/day, 4 weeks) — see HIGH PRIORITY #1 for composition; EEG sleep parameters unchanged, but shortened wake-up time after waking, trend toward better subjective sleep satisfaction and lower BDI-II depression scores (Nutrients).
- **Phuah et al. 2023** (review) — chemical composition/health benefits/quality control via chemometrics and multi-omics; notes "sufficient shading prior to harvest significantly increases theanine and chlorophyll" (mechanism only, no numbers) (Food Res Int).
- **Slawinska, Raczkowska 2026** (review) — matcha bioactive compounds and health-promoting properties across metabolic, neurological, anticancer domains; calls for more rigorous human trials (Nutrients).
- **Kosik-Bogacka, Piotrowska 2025** (review) — matcha/tea catechins and MASLD (fatty liver disease); EGCG and matcha extracts show benefit on weight, cholesterol, liver metabolism in both human and animal studies, "even in short-period experiments"; notes ~25% of global population affected by MASLD (Nutrients).
- **Ogata et al. 2026** (mouse allergic rhinitis model) — hot-water matcha extract (250 mg/kg) suppressed allergen/histamine-induced sneezing via direct downregulation of the sneeze reflex at the brainstem (Sp5C) level, independent of IgE/mast-cell/T-cell pathways; inflammatory cell counts and IgE unchanged (NPJ Sci Food).
- **Luo et al. 2024** — matcha alleviates high-fat-diet obesity in mice via gut microbiota/metabolite modulation (formononetin, glutamic acid, pyroglutamic acid, taurochenodeoxycholate); reduced body weight, adiposity, cholesterol/LDL; improved glucose tolerance; shifted Alloprevotella/Ileibacterium/Rikenella up, Romboutsia down (Curr Res Food Sci).
- **Nakao et al. 2024** — see HIGH PRIORITY #2; also clinically, matcha mouthwash reduced *P. gingivalis* counts in periodontitis patients with a trend toward improved shallow-pocket depth (Microbiology Spectrum).
- **Chong et al. 2025** (rat cafeteria-diet NAFLD model, 48 rats, 0.2%/1% matcha) — both doses improved obesity, lipid accumulation, insulin resistance, inflammatory markers; increased Akkermansia/Faecalibacterium/Parabacteroides over 12 weeks (Nutrients).
- **Iwai et al. 2021** — matcha, vitamin K1, and lutein enhanced angiogenic potential (aortic ring/tube formation assays) and preserved cortical capillary density in aging mice, suggesting a brain-vascular-aging protective role (J Nutr Sci Vitaminol).
- **Kim et al. 2020** (HFD mouse model) — matcha extract improved glucose tolerance, reduced dyslipidemia/adipose inflammation, and improved cognitive performance (Y-maze, passive avoidance, Morris water maze) via cholinergic, antioxidant, mitochondrial, and BDNF/amyloid-beta pathways (Oxid Med Cell Longev).
- **Kurauchi et al. 2023** (mouse tail-suspension test, oral matcha 10/30/100 mg/kg) — antidepressant-like effect via dopamine D1 receptor activation in prefrontal cortex/nucleus accumbens, but effect only appeared in stress-vulnerable (C57BL/6J) mice under social isolation stress, not stress-tolerant BALB/c mice (Nutrients).
- **Wang et al. 2022** (mouse HFD model, 1.0% matcha, 8 weeks) — reduced obesity/hepatic steatosis via gut-liver axis; restored fecal bile-acid profile and gut microbiota; regulated glucose/lipid/bile-acid gene expression (Front Nutr).
- **Sokary et al. 2023** (zebrafish xenograft, TNBC lines MDA-MB-468/231) — safe concentrations 50–100 µg/mL; dose-dependent reduction in tumor size and visible suppression of metastasis (Nutrients).
- **Gadekar et al. 2025** (RCT, n=32, gingivitis/periodontitis) — herbal matcha mouthrinse comparable to 2% chlorhexidine gluconate for reducing gingival inflammation; matcha also effective in vitro against *P. gingivalis* and *S. mutans* (J Contemp Dent Pract).
- **Baba, Kaneko, Takihara 2021** (RCT, n=42, 2 g/day × 2 weeks, acute psychological stress via Uchida-Kraepelin test) — matcha group had faster Stroop reaction time and better emotion-perception accuracy post-stress; no effect on fatigue or other cognitive measures (Nutrition Research).
- **Zhou et al. 2020** (HFD mouse model, matcha ethanol extracts, BV-2 microglial cells) — suppressed hypothalamic microglial activation and JAK2/STAT3 inflammatory signaling; reduced weight gain, fat accumulation, glucose/lipid abnormalities (Food Funct).
- **Sasagawa et al. 2021** — matcha supernatant (20 mg/mL) bactericidal against *S. pneumoniae* regardless of resistance status; EGCG/EGC/ECG inhibited pneumolysin oligomerization and protected neutrophils from toxin-mediated death (Antibiotics).
- **Huang et al. 2025** — matcha contains diverse miRNAs (new class of bioactive component) that vary by cultivar and harvest season; matcha had the highest levels of 4 representative miRNAs among 27 plant powders tested; positively correlated with polyphenol content (Food Chem Mol Sci).
- **Hunjadi et al. 2021** (rabbit model, 1% matcha, 20 weeks, HFD) — **negative result**: matcha impaired HDL function/cholesterol efflux capacity, reduced CETP-mediated cholesterol ester transfer, increased pulse wave velocity (arterial stiffness) — contradicts assumed cardiovascular benefit in this model (Mol Nutr Food Res).
- **Rusak, Sola, Vujcic Bok 2021** — see HIGH PRIORITY #5; matcha shows higher antidiabetic (α-glucosidase inhibition) activity than sencha through digestion.
- **Abood et al. 2025** (n=27, matcha tea 2×/day × 30 days, localized gingivitis) — improved gingival health (plaque index, bleeding on probing) and reduced salivary oxidative stress markers; baseline BOP <18% predicted successful improvement (Oral Health Prev Dent).
- **Zhou et al. 2021** (HFD mouse model, 0.1/0.5/1.0% matcha, 6 weeks) — reduced visceral/hepatic lipid, glucose, dyslipidemia, steatohepatitis; reduced TNF-α/IL-6/IL-1β; histology confirmed reduced hepatic lipid/inflammation (Nutrients).
- **Hamed, Taha, Khateeb 2023** (Wistar rats, gamma irradiation) — matcha-derived silver nanoparticles (biogenic synthesis) improved biochemical parameters, activated SIRT1, suppressed NLRP3/IL-1β, and restored splenic histology post-irradiation (Cell Biochem Funct).
- **Kiriacos et al. 2022** (review) — matcha framed as "tri-acting" agent (antitumor, immunomodulatory, anti-SARS-CoV-2); EGCG inhibits viral proteases and inflammatory pathways; quercetin, chlorophyll, caffeine, rutin, vitamin C cited as contributing compounds (Front Oncol).
- **Takegami et al. 2021** (study protocol only, double-blind crossover, healthy Japanese adults) — proposes testing two matcha types over 4-week periods for gut microbiota/dysbiosis effects; no results yet published (J Clin Biochem Nutr).
- **Kim/Baba 2018 (Unno et al.)** — see HIGH PRIORITY #8; stress-reduction depends on the (caffeine+EGCG):(theanine+arginine) molar ratio being <2; human trial (n=39) showed significantly lower anxiety with matcha vs placebo (Nutrients).
- **Hu et al. 2026** (bibliometric-style review, "coffee to matcha") — positions matcha as sustainable coffee substitute; matcha said to have higher catechins/theanine/caffeine than coffee (qualitative only, no numbers) (J Sci Food Agric).
- **Mashaqbeh et al. 2026** (rat pharmacokinetics) — matcha (250 mg/kg single dose or 21-day repeated dose) had negligible effect on most pharmacokinetic parameters of nadolol (a P-gp substrate beta-blocker) except a significant increase in half-life with repeated dosing (4.0±1.6h → 7.7±4.2h, p=.039) — a drug-interaction caution relevant to a future "interactions" note (PLoS One).
- **Cheng, Yang, Tarng 2021** — brief case report, "matcha-like ascites"; no abstract available; not matcha-composition relevant, purely a visual/clinical description analogy in a dialysis patient (Ther Apher Dial).

## Production/quality science

- **Wang, Zou, Yin 2024** — method to prevent discoloration in matcha noodles using whey protein (0.08 g/mL) + carboxymethyl chitosan (0.04 g/mL) embedding plus microwave-treated flour (700W/60s); achieved 72.13% reduction in discoloration without affecting taste/aroma (JoVE).
- **Hu et al. 2024** — hyperspectral imaging (Vis-NIR/NIR) + machine learning (XGBoost, PLSR) for non-destructive matcha quality/grade assessment; grade classification accuracy 98.10%, PLSR test R² >0.95; analyzed catechins, free amino acids, caffeine, soluble protein, soluble sugars (no absolute values given in abstract) (Food Res Int).
- **Luo et al. 2023 (regional Chinese matcha)** — see HIGH PRIORITY #9; RTA ratio and volatile-compound profiling used to characterize matcha quality/origin differences across Chinese regions and vs. Japanese matcha (Front Nutr).
- **Rezaeian, Zimmermann 2021** — developed and validated a simplified flavanol-extraction method (ethanol:water 7:3 v/v, room temp, ultrasonication) as fast/low-energy alternative to the ISO 14502-2 standard method; validated on Japanese matcha and powdered green tea, but abstract gives no absolute flavanol values (Food Chemistry).
- **Luo et al. 2022** (Shandong matcha aroma) — HS-SPME and SAFE-GC/MS identified heterocyclics (45.84–65.35%), terpenoids (7.44–16.92%), esters (6.91–15.27%) as dominant volatile classes; high-grade matcha showed seaweed-like/fresh/roasted notes vs. grassy/high-fired notes in lower grades; grinding process increased key aroma compounds including safranal and fenchyl acetate (Foods).
- **Shi et al. 2020** — matcha used as natural microencapsulant for tuna (omega-3) oil via spray-drying; matcha-only powder had superior oxidative stability vs. maltodextrin-containing formulations over 12 weeks at 40°C; loss of tea catechins significantly correlated (p<0.01) with retained EPA/DHA, i.e., catechins actively protected the omega-3s (Food Res Int).
- **Sargsyan et al. 2021** — matcha + iron(II)-lactate as a natural tannin-mordant hair dye; color intensity scales with matcha/mordant concentration; wash-fastness and hair-damage (cysteic acid marker) comparable to commercial permanent hair dyes (Molecules) — non-food application, low relevance beyond general polyphenol/tannin chemistry interest.
- **Kika et al. 2024** — see HIGH PRIORITY #3; full compositional/fatty-acid profiling methodology.
- **Kolackova et al. 2020** — see HIGH PRIORITY #4; nutritional/phenolic composition analysis methodology.
- **Jakubczyk et al. 2022 (fluoride)** — see HIGH PRIORITY #7; brewing-temperature and harvest-time effects on extractable fluoride.
- **Jakubczyk et al. 2020** — see HIGH PRIORITY #6; infusion-based antioxidant/composition methodology at varying temperatures.
