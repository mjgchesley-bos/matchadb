## HIGH PRIORITY — new compound/quantitative data

**1. Chlorophyll: Matcha has the highest chlorophyll content of any green tea type, measured directly.**
Herrera, Viera & Roca (2022), *Molecules* — "HPLC-MS Analysis of Chlorophylls in Green Teas Establishes Differences among Varieties." HPLC-MS² across 6 green tea varieties identified up to 17 chlorophyll compounds. **"Shadow teas (Matcha and Sencha) and light-regimen green teas can be statistically differentiated by the total chlorophyll content and the a/b ratio,"** and critically: **"only Matcha tea contains a higher proportion of chlorophylls a and b among the green tea varieties analyzed, justifying the higher quality and price of this variety."** First report of novel oxidized chlorophyll derivatives (132-hydroxy-chlorophylls etc.) in green tea. This is a direct, citable, quantitative-comparison paper for a chlorophyll claim on matcha product pages — no exact mg/g given in the abstract, but the a/b ratio and "highest of all varieties" claim is strong and citable.

**2. Chlorophyll degradation: Matcha and Sencha show similar pheophorbide-a build-up; chlorophyllase activity drops ~20x after processing.**
Kohata et al. (1998), *Biosci Biotechnol Biochem* — "Pheophorbide a Content and Chlorophyllase Activity in Green Tea." **"The total content of PB a [pheophorbide a] markedly increased in both Sencha and Matcha, compared with the initially present PB a content in each"** during incubation. **Chlorophyllase activity ratio: Sencha : fresh green leaves ≈ 1:20** — i.e., processing reduces but does not eliminate the enzyme that degrades chlorophyll to pheophorbide a. Directly relevant to why matcha color fades/browns over time and to any shelf-life/quality claims.

**3. Matcha-style stone-milling triples extractable EGCG versus leaf tea.**
Fujioka et al. (2016), *Molecules* — "The Powdering Process with a Set of Ceramic Mills for Green Tea Promoted Catechin Extraction and the ROS Inhibition Effect." **"The powdering process with a ceramic mill and stirring in hot water increased the average extracted concentration of epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG) by more than three times"** compared with leaf tea (measured by HPLC/LC-MS/MS). Powdered tea also showed higher in-vitro ROS-inhibition than the same mass of leaf tea. This is a strong, citable mechanistic reason matcha delivers more bioavailable EGCG per gram than steeped leaf tea — worth a callout on matcha vs. sencha comparison pages.

**4. Direct catechin quantification for "Japan Matcha" vs. other origins (mg/L, extraction study).**
Almahasheer (2024), *Brazilian Journal of Biology* — "Unveiling the total catechin profile in tea leaves... UPLC-IDX-Orbitrap MS." Using a novel one-step extraction: **lowest total catechin group = 322–346 mg/L, including "Japan Matcha"** (grouped with Vietnam and Morocco samples); **highest group = 422–424 mg/L, Sri Lanka and "Japan Sencha."** Caveat: single small pilot study (Saudi-affiliated), reproducibility RSD <15%, and "Japan Matcha" here is one commercial sample, not a grade-controlled comparison — treat as a data point, not a definitive matcha-vs-sencha catechin ranking (it actually runs opposite to the general "matcha ≥ sencha in catechins" assumption, so flag but don't overweight).

**5. Shading duration is not the only nitrogen-metabolism lever — degraded shade-net plastic residue actively suppresses theanine biosynthesis.**
Wang et al. (2026), *Environmental Science & Technology* — "Field-Aged Shade Nets Residues Disrupt Rhizosphere Microbiota and Nitrogen Metabolism in Tea." Field-aged (weathered) shade nets, versus pristine nets, caused stronger rhizosphere microbial restructuring, depleted soil available nitrogen, enriched denitrifying bacteria/saprotrophic fungi, and — the key line — **aged-net-disrupted nitrogen metabolism "suppressed glutamate and theanine biosynthesis."** This is a *counter-mechanism* worth flagging: it shows shade-net material degradation (a real-world confound in shade-grown tea, including matcha's tencha) can work against the shading→theanine benefit if nets are old/weathered. Good nuance for a rigorous compounds-methodology page.

**6. Cultivar genome comparison: 'Seimei' has lower catechins / higher amino acids / lower caffeine than Yabukita.**
Kawahara et al. (2024), *Plant & Cell Physiology* — "Chromosome-Scale Genome Assembly and Characterization of Top-Quality Japanese Green Tea Cultivar 'Seimei'." New reference genome (3.1 Gb, 15 chromosomes, N50 214.9 Mb) for the premium cultivar 'Seimei' (registered 2020), explicitly benchmarked against 'Yabukita' and 'Saemidori' — the same Yabukita already in the site's cultivar_research tier. **"Lower catechin content and higher amino acid content than those of 'Yabukita' and 'Saemidori'"**; also lower caffeine and a higher EGC/EGCG ratio (less astringent) in Japanese cultivars generally. 40 candidate genes under selection identified, including catechin-synthesis enzymes and caffeine synthase. This is directly useful as a comparator/caveat next to the existing Yabukita EGCG=77mg/g citation — it shows Yabukita is *not* the lowest-catechin/highest-amino-acid Japanese cultivar; newer cultivars have been bred to outperform it on exactly these axes.

**7. Organic acids vary systematically by tea grade (Sencha → Gyokuro → Matcha) — a shading-correlated compositional gradient.**
Shirai (2019), *J Oleo Science* — "Gas Chromatographic Analysis of Organic Acids in Japanese Green Tea Leaves." **Oxalic acid concentration increases from Sencha → Gyokuro → Matcha; citric acid increases from Sencha → Matcha → Gyokuro** (opposite ordering). Oxalic acid "only slightly increased with increasing tea grade" within Gyokuro grades. The n-3/n-6 fatty acid ratio was **low in high-grade Matcha, Gyokuro, and Sencha** and correlated with perceived green tea quality — used as a proposed quality-grading marker. Companion paper (below) adds quinic acid data.

**8. Quinic acid: Matcha lowest of tea types studied; high-grade Matcha lower than low-grade Matcha; shading directly moves the acid profile.**
Shirai (2022), *J Oleo Science* — "Organic Acid Analysis in Green Tea Leaves Using High-performance Liquid Chromatography." **"Kukicha contained the highest quinic acid levels; Matcha had the lowest."** **"High-grade Matcha showed significantly lower quinic acid than low-grade varieties"** — a rare instance of a grade-differentiated numeric organic-acid claim specific to matcha grading. Quinic acid rose with leaf maturity; malic/oxalic/succinic/citric acids rose then fell with maturity. **Shading cultivation (cultivar Saeakari) lowered quinic acid but slightly raised malic, citric, and oxalic acid** — a second, independent data point (beyond the Goto et al. theanine/EGCG numbers) that shading measurably shifts a named tea's chemical profile in a controlled cultivar comparison. Malic/citric acid in **Yabukita** varied with time of day of plucking (sunrise vs. sunset).

## Shading/theanine/catechin mechanism findings

**CsPIF7 (Li et al. 2025, *Plant, Cell & Environment*)** — "A Shading-Responsive bHLH Transcription Factor (CsPIF7) Promotes Theanine Biosynthesis in Tea Plants." **Abstract was inaccessible** (PubMed reCAPTCHA on repeated tries; Wiley publisher page returned HTTP 403). The title alone is highly relevant and should be tracked for future full-text access: it names a specific transcription factor (CsPIF7, a phytochrome-interacting-factor-family bHLH gene, consistent with light/shade signaling) as a direct positive regulator of theanine biosynthesis — exactly the kind of gene-level mechanism that would let MatchaDB cite a *mechanism* alongside the existing Goto et al. numbers. Recommend a follow-up fetch attempt via institutional access or a cached PDF.

**Fang et al. (2022), *Frontiers in Plant Science*** — "Effects of Different Shading Treatments on the Biomass and Transcriptome Profiles of Tea Leaves and the Regulatory Effect on Phytohormone Biosynthesis." Compared black, blue, and red shade nets against unshaded control. **Blue net vs. black net: bud density +2.00×, 100-bud fresh weight +1.24×, yield +2.48×.** Flavonoid composition under blue net was "comparable with black net shading." Differentially regulated pathways: phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, MAPK signaling, plant hormone signal transduction; light-sensing genes UVR8, PHYE, CRY1, PHYB, PHOT2, HY5 significantly linked to phytohormone biosynthesis genes (p<0.05); salicylic acid and melatonin correlated with light-perception genes. Conclusion explicitly ties shading regulation to **matcha production** applications.

**Ye et al. (2021), *Molecules*** — "Effects of Light Intensity and Spectral Composition on the Transcriptome Profiles of Leaves in Shade Grown Tea Plants and Regulatory Network of Flavonoid Biosynthesis." Black nets at varying %, plus blue/yellow/red nets. **Total flavonol glycosides decreased up to 79.6% under shade; total catechins decreased 38.7%** (i.e., catechins are more shade-resistant than flavonol glycosides in this dataset — a useful nuance/refinement to the blanket "shading suppresses catechins" claim, since it quantifies relative sensitivity). 29,292 unigenes profiled; structural genes *PAL*, *CHS*, *F3H* downregulated as light intensity fell; TFs *MYB12*, *MYB86*, *C1*, *MYB4*, *KTN80.4* correlated with flavonoid content; light-signal genes *UVR8*, *HY5* implicated. Abscisic acid rose under shade, negatively correlated with flavonol glycosides (p<0.05). Explicitly ties findings to matcha production.

**Li et al. (2025), *Physiologia Plantarum*** — "Different performance of tea plants to shade based on key metabolites and transcriptome profiles: case study of cultivars Longjing 43 and Yabukita." Directly compares the shading response of **Yabukita** (the site's existing cultivar-tier citation) against Longjing 43. **"Flavonol glycosides were highly responsive to shading, while catechins displayed distinct cultivar-specific responses."** **Longjing 43 maintained relatively higher epi-type catechin levels under shade than Yabukita**; genes *CsANR* and *CsANS* were less shade-sensitive in Longjing 43 than in Yabukita. UVR8-mediated light signaling showed cultivar-specific expression despite conserved function. No specific theanine/EGCG numeric values in the abstract, but this is a directly relevant cultivar-comparison paper that could nuance or caveat the site's Yabukita-only cultivar tier (i.e., Yabukita is comparatively *more* shade-responsive/catechin-suppressible than some newer cultivars).

**Wang et al. (2026)** — see HIGH PRIORITY #5 above (shade-net degradation suppresses theanine/glutamate biosynthesis via nitrogen metabolism disruption).

**Zhang et al. (2024), *Heliyon*** — "Plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) improve the growth and quality of several crops." Field trials on mature **Longjing 43** tea plantations (Guizhou). PGPR + fertilizer treatment produced measurable increases in "tea polyphenols, caffeine, and theanine" and increased chlorophyll content, "particularly important for Matcha green tea coloring" (per the fetched summary). Yield: PGPR+fertilizer combo gave +15.38% over fertilizer alone; PGPR alone gave +92.31% over no treatment. Not a shading study, but a second agronomic lever (soil microbiome) affecting the same compound set (theanine, caffeine, polyphenols, chlorophyll) — useful context for a "what actually controls these numbers" discussion.

**Shirai (2022)** organic-acid paper — see HIGH PRIORITY #8 (shading in Saeakari cultivar measurably shifted organic acid profile).

## Health effects

**Cognitive/mood:**
- Sohail et al. (2021), *Cureus* — "The Cognitive-Enhancing Outcomes of Caffeine and L-theanine: A Systematic Review." Caffeine inhibits adenosine A1/A2a receptors, boosting dopaminergic transmission/attention; L-theanine acts as a glutamate reuptake inhibitor with GABA-A-mediated neuroprotection. Full results/dosing not retrievable from the abstract excerpt.
- Cavanah et al. (2025), *Biomedicines* — "The Impact of Green Tea and Its Bioactive Compounds on Mood Disorder Symptomology and BDNF: Systematic Review of RCTs." 13 RCTs included (from 445 screened): depression improved in 4 studies, anxiety in 6, stress in 5, sleep in 1. **"Green tea, GTE, L-theanine, and EGCG may improve mood disorder symptomology, particularly symptoms of depression."** No study found a significant BDNF effect. 7 trials used isolated EGCG/L-theanine; 6 used whole green tea/matcha/GTE. No dosage/effect-size data recoverable from abstract.
- Dietz & Dekker (2017), *Curr Pharm Des* — "Effect of Green Tea Phytochemicals on Mood and Cognition." Review of 49 human intervention studies. **Caffeine improves demanding long-duration cognitive tasks and alertness "already at low doses of 40 mg."** **L-theanine alone improves relaxation/calmness "starting at 200 mg."** Combined theanine+caffeine improves attention-switching and alertness (somewhat less than caffeine alone). EGCG: "no conclusive evidence" — too few intervention studies. Good citable numeric dosing thresholds (40mg caffeine / 200mg theanine) for a future public-facing health page.
- Yoshida et al. (2024), *Life* (Brain Healthcare Quotient) — abstract inaccessible (PubMed reCAPTCHA + MDPI 403 on repeated tries). Title suggests a standardized brain-health-intervention framework; unclear if green tea/matcha is a named intervention. Flag for follow-up.
- Bayo Jimenez et al. (2025), *Int J Mol Sci* (natural products in Alzheimer's) — abstract inaccessible (repeated PubMed reCAPTCHA + MDPI 403). Title indicates a systematic review of natural products including likely green tea/EGCG in Alzheimer's clinical trials and mechanisms — worth a follow-up fetch given direct relevance to a future cognitive-health page.
- Sugita & Furukawa (2022), *Neuroscience* — "Effect of Green Tea and Tea Catechin on the Visual Motion Processing for Optokinetic Responses in Mice." Mice given matcha powder or sencha extract for 1 month, then IP EGCG: **"OKRs of young and aging mice after green tea intake and after EGCG administration showed higher temporal sensitivity than control mice"** — enhanced motion detection in both age groups, attributed to EGCG. No dosage given in abstract.
- Kim et al. (2024), *J Microbiol Biotechnol* — "Green Tea Attenuates the PM-Exposed Gut-Brain Axis Dysfunction..." Matcha extract in PM2.5-exposed mice: improved colon length/SCFA production, strengthened gut barrier, reduced inflammation/oxidative stress; regulated *Bacteroides*, *Ruminococcus*, *Murinobaculaceae* affecting tryptophan/hormone metabolism; normalized tryptophan-serotonin axis; reduced brain COX-2 and acetylcholinesterase activity. Mouse model only; no dosage in abstract. Directly relevant to a stress/cognitive matcha health page (gut-brain axis mechanism).
- Unno et al. (2024), *Nutrients* — "Stress-Relieving Effects of Green Tea Depend on the Ratio of Its Special Ingredients and the Infusion Conditions." Abstract inaccessible (repeated PubMed reCAPTCHA + MDPI 403). Title is exactly on-point for a future page combining theanine/caffeine ratio and brewing-condition guidance — strongly recommend a manual follow-up read since this could yield the second, independent theanine:caffeine ratio dataset the site might want.

**Antiviral/other disease:**
- Shin-Ya et al. (2023), *Scientific Reports* — EGCG, GCG (gallocatechin gallate), and TFDG (black tea theaflavin) inhibited Omicron subvariants BA.1 and XE via spike RBD binding; effectiveness reduced against BA.2.75 (mutations N460K/G446S/F490S). Whole green tea, **matcha**, and black tea all inactivated virus in vitro; saliva from tea-candy consumers showed reduced viral infectivity ex vivo.
- Sato (1999), *Int J Urology* — green tea (esp. green tea leaves) dose-dependently inhibited BBN-induced bladder tumor growth in rats when given after carcinogen exposure; catechin implicated as active component.
- Li et al. (2021), *Oxid Med Cell Longev* — 10 green teas tested in alcohol-induced fatty liver disease mice, including **"Seven Star Matcha Tea"** and **"Selenium-Enriched Matcha Tea."** At 200 mg/kg, top performers (incl. Selenium-Enriched Matcha) reduced serum/liver triglycerides and aminotransferases, decreased CYP2E1 expression, lowered lipid peroxidation and pro-inflammatory cytokines. Active compounds implicated: gallic acid, gallocatechin, catechin, chlorogenic acid, EGCG.
- Conde et al. (2026), *Dentistry Journal* — "Green Tea in the Prevention and Treatment of Dry Mouth: A Review." 18 studies reviewed; 15/18 (83%) showed improvement in at least one xerostomia-related outcome. **"Green tea improved SFR [salivary flow rate] and salivary pH more consistently than black, oolong, or matcha tea"** — notably, matcha underperformed plain green tea here. Authors call for larger standardized trials.
- Fu et al. (2020), *Food & Function* — matcha added to cooked rice (3%) reduced rapidly-digestible starch from 72.96% to 60.99% and estimated glycemic index from 77.55 to 66.86; mechanism via increased ordered starch crystal structure. A metabolic-health-relevant, matcha-specific numeric result, though about food-matrix interaction rather than matcha alone.
- Akif et al. (2026), *Food Sci Nutr* (dietary polyphenols, non-communicable disease) — abstract inaccessible (repeated PubMed reCAPTCHA + Wiley 403). Broad polyphenol review; likely includes green tea catechins among reviewed compounds but unconfirmed.
