## ISO/TR 21380:2022 — the ceremonial-grade question

**This is the key deliverable of this batch.** The CiNii Research page for 角川修・谷口郁也 (2022) "抹茶の国際標準化に向けた取り組み: 技術報告書ISO/TR 21380:2022「抹茶：定義と特徴」" (JATAFFジャーナル, Sept. 2022) exposed only bibliographic metadata — no abstract text. So I went to the source: I located and downloaded the official ISO preview PDF (`cdn.standards.iteh.ai/samples/80777/.../ISO-TR-21380-2022.pdf`, the standards.iteh.ai "iTeh Standard Preview" sample) and extracted it with pypdf. This preview covers the *entire normative body of the document* (Scope, Normative References, Terms & Definitions, Origins, Cultivation, Processing, Grinding, Sensory Analysis, Shelf Life, and the start of §5 Characteristics) — it cuts off right as §5.2 "Chemical characteristics" begins, which is the one part I could not retrieve (that section, with any numeric compound ranges, is presumably in the paid full document; I could not find its contents reproduced anywhere else on the web).

### Direct answer

**ISO/TR 21380:2022 does NOT define, recognize, or use the terms "ceremonial grade" or "culinary grade," or any equivalent formal grade-tier system.** It defines matcha almost purely by **plant source + production method**, with only an informal two-way distinction based on process fidelity ("high quality" vs. "normal quality/normal grade") — not a marketed tier system.

### What the standard actually says (verbatim quotes from the extracted text)

**Formal definition (§3.1, Terms and definitions):**
> "matcha tea: tea derived solely and exclusively from harvested tender leaves, buds and shoots of varieties of the species *Camellia sinensis* (L.) O. Kuntze var. *sinensis* which are grown under shade, known to be suitable for making tea for consumption as a beverage, and produced by acceptable processes notably enzyme inactivation using a steaming process followed by a drying process, without rolling the leaves, and a fine grinding process for the leaf to make tea powder"

**Scope (§1):** "This document contains information regarding the tea cultivation, manufacture (including processing and grinding) and sensory analysis of the tea referred to as 'matcha tea' in international trade. It provides an internationally agreed definition of matcha tea based on the plant source, cultivation and production methods used... It does not apply to flavoured matcha tea, blended matcha tea, or decaffeinated or soluble extracts of matcha tea."

**Particle size (§4.4.2 and §5.1, the closest thing to a hard number in the document):** "The mean particle size of matcha tea powder is in the range 10 μm to 30 μm with a median diameter of less than 20 μm." This single particle-size band applies to matcha as a whole — it is NOT split into a fine "ceremonial" band and a coarser "culinary" band. (Third-party marketing sources like chtmatcha.com claim ISO specifies "D50 of 5-10 μm for ceremonial" — this is NOT in the ISO text; that site's own analysis explicitly says those numbers come from "industry benchmarks and their own testing experience," not from ISO/TR 21380.)

**The only tiering language in the whole document (§4.4.1-4.4.2), and it's about *process fidelity*, not marketed grade names:**
> "The broad quality range of matcha tea, including high quality, follows a precise leaf-drying process using a classic brick wall tencha leaf dryer. **Normal quality grade matcha tea** is also produced with some flexibility in processing which can include the use of metal dryers."
> "The high quality matcha tea is produced using a tencha (tea) grinder (a special stone mill designed to grind tencha leaves)... **Normal grade matcha tea** can use some grinding processes other than the stone mill method, which can include a ceramic ball mill or a jet mill."

So the ISO document's only "grade" language is: **stone-mill + brick tencha-ki dryer = "high quality"; ball-mill/jet-mill + metal dryer = "normal grade."** This maps loosely onto what Western retailers call "ceremonial" vs. "culinary," but ISO never uses those words, and it frames it as a production-method spectrum/quality continuum rather than a formal named certification tier.

**Introduction (context for why the standard exists):** "There is some confusion as to the origins, cultivation, appearance and processing for matcha tea and how this can or does differ from green, black, white and other types of tea... An investigation of the levels of L-theanine, chlorophyll and catechins — including epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG) and epigallocatechin (EGC) — in matcha tea indicates that the levels can vary [from those] in green and black teas and shows they support the discrimination between matcha, green and black teas." Note: this is about distinguishing *matcha from other tea types* (green/black/white), not about ranking matcha internally into grades. The promised chemical-characteristics numbers for that discrimination are in §5.2, which I could not access (see below).

**Other definitional facts worth noting for MatchaDB:**
- Cultivation: two shading methods, "roof frame shading" (棚) and "direct shading" — roof shading uses a two-step light reduction (70-80% then 95-98% transmission-blocking) over 20-30 days; direct shading cuts 85% of sunlight, plucked after 20 days (14 days for second flush).
- Processing: leaves steamed 15-30 s at 100°C in a rotating drum, then dried in a "tencha-ki" dryer at 150-220°C air temp (leaf temp ~70°C) over 3-5 stages on perforated belt conveyors.
- Grinding room temperature is controlled to ~20-25°C to prevent color degradation from frictional heat.
- Shelf life: quality deteriorates from light, temperature, humidity, oxygen exposure; color shifts bright green → olive green as chlorophyll degrades to pheophytin; recommends sealed aluminum foil pouches with nitrogen flushing, storage at ≤5°C.
- Sensory evaluation protocol given in the standard: 2 g matcha + 100 mL boiling water in a tasting bowl, whisked, judged on color/particle fineness/foaming/foam color/umami — this exact protocol (2 g / 100 mL) recurs across multiple Japanese papers in this batch (see #215 below), suggesting it's a de facto national standard method, not ISO-specific.

**Gap flagged for future research:** ISO/TR 21380:2022 §5.2 "Chemical characteristics" almost certainly contains the promised L-theanine/chlorophyll/catechin/EGCG/EGC discriminating ranges. I could not retrieve this section — the free iTeh "standard preview" PDF cuts off exactly at the start of §5.2, and no third-party source I found reproduces it. If MatchaDB wants those numbers, the paid ISO document itself (CHF, via iso.org/standard/80777.html) is the only way to get them; a NARO press release (naro.go.jp/english/laboratory/nifts/press/matcha) confirms NARO's stated intent to build a *follow-on* standard specifically defining chemical-component ranges, implying even ISO itself considers this open/未完成 as of the 2022 TR.

Sources consulted: ISO/TR 21380:2022 preview PDF (cdn.standards.iteh.ai); iso.org/standard/80777.html; naro.go.jp NIFTS press release; blog.ansi.org summary; chtmatcha.com (third-party, used only to confirm what is NOT in ISO); CiNii Research citation page for the Kakukawa/Taniguchi commentary paper (metadata only, no abstract accessible).

## HIGH PRIORITY — new compound/quantitative data

All numbers below are extracted directly from full text (not abstracts) unless noted otherwise.

**Grade/price vs. compound content (the recurring theme across multiple papers):**
- Ikegaya et al. 1984 (茶研報No.60, entry #223): compared matcha at 5 price tiers (¥5000/4000/3000/2000/1000 per 40g). Total nitrogen, caffeine, free amino acids, and theanine all **decreased with decreasing price**. Arginine, aspartic acid, glutamic acid, threonine, theanine specifically lower in cheaper grades. Vitamin C higher in grades >¥3000/40g than <¥2000/40g. Free reducing sugars and Vitamin E showed little price-related variation.
- Horie 2018 review (entry #212) citing Goto et al. 1994/1996 (the same Tea Research Report papers already used as MatchaDB's Tier-3 citation): comparing upper/middle/lower commercial matcha classes, total N/free amino acids/caffeine were higher in upper-grade, **catechins were higher in lower-grade** — confirms the existing MatchaDB grade_research direction.
- Horie et al. (cited within #212, ref. 6): competition-winning tencha had EGC content of just **0.44%**, vs. **3.8%** in upper-grade commercial sencha — EGC is dramatically suppressed in the best tencha.
- Horie et al. 2017/2018 tencha price-series study (cited in #212, its own JSTAGE entry is #222): tencha priced ¥1,000–¥30,000/kg (cheapest harvested in autumn) — **EGCG showed little difference across price tiers, but EGC was ~3% in low-grade vs. ~1% or less in high-grade tencha.** Proposed **EGCG/EGC ratio as a matcha quality indicator**. Same study: higher-priced tencha had higher free amino acids (theanine, arginine).
- Entry #222 (Horie, Ema, Sumikawa 2017, 日本調理科学会誌 50(5):182-188 — this is citation #8 in Horie's 2018 review and also the source paper cited by MatchaDB's own compounds-extract.mjs pipeline potentially worth cross-checking): WebFetch's own English paraphrase reported "ceremonial matcha" theanine ≥1.8 g/100g dry weight, EGCG/EGC ratio ≥3.2, chlorophyll-a >250 mg/100g dry weight, distinguishing tea-ceremony-grade matcha from commercial powdered green tea (except gyokuro powder, which is comparable). **Caution: "ceremonial" here is the WebFetch summarizer's own translation choice, not a confirmed literal term from the Japanese original — flagging for verification, not for direct citation, until the original text is checked.**

**Vitamin/mineral/chlorophyll data:**
- Vitamin E (α-tocopherol): sencha/gyokuro 55-72 mg/100g and 24 mg/100g respectively, vs. **matcha 24-36 mg/100g**; matcha and sencha/gyokuro differ in tocopherol *composition* too — sencha shows β- and γ-tocopherol, matcha shows almost none but α-tocopherol (Ikegaya et al. 1984, cited in #212).
- Vitamin K: 1 g of matcha supplies ~19% of the Japanese RDI; vitamin K content **increases under shading cultivation** (cited in #212).
- 1989 multi-tea-type study (entry #233): Vitamin C — sencha avg 167 mg/100g; **matcha ranged widely, 12-367 mg/100g** (i.e., grade/processing-dependent, consistent with #223's price-tier finding). Tocopherol: matcha & sencha averaged 13.5 mg/100g. β-carotene avg 3.2 mg/100g (detected in green tea/matcha/genmaicha); α-carotene avg 27.9 mg/100g (detected across nearly all tea types). Chlorophyll-carotenoid correlation r=0.912 in sencha. Conclusion: brewed infusions contain negligible amounts of these fat-soluble compounds — "direct leaf consumption, such as matcha, is necessary" to obtain them.
- Mineral content of matcha (Haraguchi 2015, entry #214): K 2.7%, Ca 420 mg%, Mg 230 mg%, Mn 3.3 mg%, Al 2.8 mg%, Fe 17 mg%, Zn 0.6 mg%, Na 0.6 mg%; fluoride notably high vs. other plants at 8.9-21.9 mg% (complexed with aluminum). Protein content ~30% (high for a plant food) — matcha's insoluble protein yields an ACE-inhibitory peptide with blood-pressure-lowering activity (Yoshikawa, cited).
- Storage/chlorophyll kinetics (Haraguchi et al. 2002, entry #227 — raw matcha stored at -70/4/25/37°C for 24 weeks): Chl-a residual at 24 wk: -70°C 94.0%, 4°C 83.0%, 25°C 67.4%, **37°C 33.7%**. Chl-b more stable: -70°C 100.4%, 4°C 93.7%, 25°C 83.7%, 37°C 71.0%. Vitamin C residual at 24 wk: 37°C 72.7%, 25°C 82.0%. Recommend storage ≤25°C for 24-week shelf stability; at 37°C usable life only 3-4 weeks.
- Tencha long-term aging storage (entry #232): Vitamin C retention ~80% under air-permeable packaging vs. ~90% under nitrogen-flush packaging; chlorophyll content/color stable with no significant change; **chlorophyllase activity did not decline even after 6 months** of storage — attributed to tencha's low moisture content keeping the enzyme inactive.
- Chlorophyll separation method paper (1957, entry #231): gyokuro retains chlorophyll a/b largely undegraded; sencha/roasted tea retain only ~30% of chlorophyll-b, chlorophyll-a mostly degraded; black tea shows complete degradation of both. Degradation products identified as pheophorbide-a/b.

**Matcha latte / stress study exact compound table (Monobe et al. 2021, entry #213) — per daily dose:**

| Compound | Matcha latte | Hojicha latte (control) |
|---|---|---|
| EGCG | 177.0 mg | 1.2 mg |
| EGC | 54.0 mg | 0.6 mg |
| Theanine | 48.7 mg | 0.0 mg |
| Arginine | 11.2 mg | 0.0 mg |
| Caffeine | 81.0 mg | 4.8 mg |

Molar ratio (caffeine+EGCG)/(theanine+arginine) for this matcha latte = **2.3** — above the ≥2 threshold Unno et al. (2018, cited within) found abolishes matcha's stress-protective effect. This ratio concept recurs as the paper's main practical finding: a nominally "high quality"/high-grade matcha latte still failed to protect against acute stress because its ratio exceeded 2.

**Particle size / grinding-method data (the largest cluster of quantitative findings in this batch):**
- Entry #216 (Haraguchi et al. 2003): normal (ball-mill) matcha particle range 1-100 μm, median 11.5 μm; fine matcha (jet-milled or air-classified) narrowed to 1-20 μm, median **3.2 μm**. Fine matcha in 1% suspension sedimented only 0.9 mL/100mL after 240 min vs. 7.0 mL/100mL for normal matcha (~8x less). Bread-rise test: no matcha 11.1 cm, normal matcha added 7.9 cm (71.6% of control), fine matcha added 11.2 cm (101.6% — full recovery of rise).
- Entry #217: coarse (stone-mill/ball-mill) matcha median 15-20 μm, max ≥50 μm; fine/jet-mill matcha median ≤5 μm, max ≤10 μm; jet-mill matcha spreadability >2x coarse matcha.
- Entry #218: stone-mill matcha showed poor flowability specifically due to **electrostatic charging correlated with ambient humidity** — a novel processing-science finding (antistatic equipment recommended).
- Entry #219 (Sawamura et al. 2012, foam/bubble diameter — full methodology paper): median diameters measured: coarse matcha 19.1 μm, stone-mill matcha 18.3 μm, ball-mill matcha 17.5 μm, fine matcha 4.6 μm, jet-mill matcha 2.9 μm. Foam volume ratio (finest:coarsest) = 2.2x immediately after whisking, 2.0x at 10 min. Foam bubble diameter itself did NOT differ much by grinding method (0.24-0.34 mm immediately, 0.55-0.92 mm at 10 min) — matcha particles were shown by microscopy to **localize specifically on the inner surface of the bubble film**, reinforcing/stabilizing it. Tencha aqueous extract alone (no particles) foamed like coarse matcha — i.e., soluble saponin sets a baseline foam level, and fine particle count adds the extra foaming on top.
- Entry #220: fine matcha median 6.84 μm vs. coarse 21.56 μm; concentrations tested 0.5-3.5%; foam stability increased above 2% concentration, most pronounced in fine powder.
- Entry #221 (Sawamura 2019, palatability): stone-mill and ball-mill matcha both ~10-20 μm median but differ in size *distribution*; jet-mill/classified fine powder 3-5 μm. Stone-mill matcha uniquely combines texture *and* smoothness; ball-mill sometimes gritty; jet-milled feels smooth but lacks any "texture" sensation at all — implying some particle-size heterogeneity is desirable for palatability, not just "as fine as possible."
- Entry #238 (Sawamura 2011, pre-medieval matcha): recreated matcha ground with a mortar/pestle (薬研, used until ~1300) vs. stone mill (used after). Mortar-ground matcha had median particle size **~150 μm** (coarse, gritty, bitter); stone-mill-ground matcha ~10 μm (fine, smooth, umami-forward). This is a striking historical-production data point: matcha's characteristic fine grittless texture is a technology artifact of the stone mill's adoption, not an ancient constant.
- Historical particle-size data compiled in Horie's review (#212): stone-mill matcha bimodal distribution with peaks at ~3-6 μm and ~20-30 μm (thin leaf parts ground fine, thick veins/midribs ground coarse); average matcha (drinking grade) median 5.7 μm via laser diffraction; foreign (non-Japanese) matcha tends to show a bimodal particle distribution while Japanese-made matcha tends toward a single peak (cited from Nakamura 2018 comparative data) — a potential country-of-origin discriminator.
- Roughness-perception threshold: entry #215 (Murakami 2022) found the sensory "grittiness" boundary at median diameter **16.0 μm** in a 74-sample matcha panel (binary logistic regression, P<0.001), consistent with the general human oral roughness-perception threshold of 10-25 μm cited from food-texture literature.
- Whisking/extraction chemistry (entry #228): during standard whisking preparation, theanine/caffeine/EGC extract at >80% into the liquor, but **EGCG extracts at ≤50%** — explained by EGCG's temperature-dependent solubility dropping as the whisked water rapidly cools; this is proposed as the reason prepared matcha tastes less bitter than its dry-leaf composition would predict. Foaming itself was shown NOT to meaningfully change pre/post-whisk compound concentration (i.e., the foam doesn't skim off astringent compounds as folk theory held).

**Aroma chemistry (mostly non-quantitative but names specific compounds):**
- Dimethyl sulfide (DMS) is the primary "covering aroma" (覆い香) marker of shaded cultivation, generated from precursor methylmethionine sulfonium (MMS) during heat processing; correlates positively with sensory aroma scores and price (#214, #212).
- Entry #224 (olfactory-receptor + GC profiling of 6 matcha varieties): identified 2-isopropyl-3-methoxypyrazine as a characteristic compound; used an ~3,000-compound olfactory receptor activity database plus GC-O/GC-MS/PCA to cluster matcha varieties by aroma signature.
- Entry #225 (Japanese vs. foreign matcha aroma discriminators, 2021, 茶研報132): compounds elevated in Japanese vs. foreign matcha include dimethyl sulfide, Strecker-derived methyl aldehydes (2-methylpropanal, 3-methylbutanal, 2-methylbutanal), and — identified via PCA as distinctively Japanese — 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline and furaneol. Framed as both heat-generated (processing) and shading/fertilization-management-derived (cultivation) markers — i.e., a chemical fingerprint that could in principle authenticate Japanese-origin matcha.
- Historically noted characteristic matcha-specific (not just "covered tea") aroma compound: trans-4,5-epoxy-(E)-2-decenal, attributed specifically to the tencha-machine + stone-mill process (Baba et al., cited in #212) — i.e., distinct from the shading-driven DMS/carotenoid aromas, this one comes from the *grinding/processing* step itself.

**Korean shading-method study (entry #244, Hadong Green Tea Research Institute, J. Korean Tea Society 2019 25(1):88-96) — direct numeric comparison of 3 shading methods on matcha:**
- Direct shading: highest total phenolics (141.6 mg GAE/g) and strongest antioxidant activity, highest EGCG.
- FRP-pole shading: highest theanine, caffeine, and ECG.
- Screen shading: best color quality (G value 49.3, chroma 34.7, described as comparable to premium Japanese matcha color standards), highest chlorophyll and sugar content, but lowest caffeine.
- Electronic-tongue taste profiling: screen-shaded matcha read as more astringent; direct-shaded read with stronger umami aftertaste/bitterness.
- This is a genuine controlled experiment isolating **shading method** (not just shading duration) as an independent variable affecting compound profile — useful as a non-Japan-specific reference point.

**Chinese particle-size/processing studies:**
- Entry #246 (Zhang Hui et al. 2019, 茶叶科学 39(4):464-473, ball-mill grinding time series): median particle diameter reduced from 117.094 to 14.209 μm across grinding times tested. Tea polyphenols and free amino acids **increased then declined** with grinding time (non-monotonic). Ascorbic acid and EGCG content **decreased with prolonged grinding**. Brightness increased and green tone intensified with finer particles. Bulk density decreased with increasing porosity; particle agglomeration increased at finer sizes due to higher surface energy.
- Entry #247 (ultrafine grinding technology review, 2021, Food Industry magazine): cites a Chinese industry definition of matcha as **D60 ≤ 18 μm**; traditional stone-ground matcha ~106.0 μm; modern ultrafine grinding achieves 10.0-32.0 μm; states ultrafine grinding technology broadly can reach 1.0-100.0 μm (micron), 0.1-1.0 μm (submicron), or 0.001-0.1 μm (nano) scale. Notes China now produces an estimated ~70% of global matcha supply (industry-context claim, not independently verified here).

## Grade/classification findings

This batch strongly reinforces that **"ceremonial" / "culinary" as fixed, industry-standard category names are a Western retail invention, not a term used in Japanese primary/industry sources** — but there IS a real underlying production-method distinction that these marketing terms are loosely mapping onto.

1. **ISO/TR 21380:2022** — see full section above. No "ceremonial"/"culinary" terms; uses "high quality" vs. "normal quality grade" tied strictly to grinding tool (stone mill vs. ball/jet mill) and dryer type (traditional brick tencha-ki vs. metal dryer). Single particle-size band (10-30 μm mean, <20 μm median) for matcha as a whole, not tiered.

2. **Japan Tea Central Association (日本茶業中央会) formal definition**, quoted verbatim in two independent papers in this batch (Horie 2018, entry #212; Sawamura et al. 2012, entry #219): "覆下栽培した茶葉を揉まずに乾燥した茶葉（碾茶）を茶臼で挽いて微粉状に製造したもの" (tea leaves grown under cover, dried without rolling [= tencha], ground into fine powder with a stone mill) = matcha. Critically: **"被覆栽培した茶葉であってもボールミル等で粉砕した場合は、『粉末茶』として抹茶とは区別して記載した"** — even shade-grown tea, if ground with a ball mill (not a stone mill), is by this official definition called "**funmatcha**" (powdered tea), explicitly NOT matcha. This is a hard binary (grinding-tool-based) distinction in the actual Japanese regulatory/industry language — closer to ISO's high-quality/normal-grade split than to a ceremonial/culinary binary, and it's about stone-mill vs. non-stone-mill, not about end-use (drinking vs. cooking).

3. **Real-world industry terminology found in the 2025 Uji tea-merchant study (entry #236, フードシステム研究 32(3)):** Uji tea wholesalers internally distinguish **点前用 ("for-whisking-use," i.e. tea-ceremony use) vs. 加工用 ("for-processing-use")** tencha/matcha — this is the actual operative Japanese trade distinction, and it is the real ancestor concept behind the Western "ceremonial vs. culinary" marketing split. Notably the paper documents a *structural industry shift*: several century-old Uji tea merchants now report processing-grade demand at 50-75% of total matcha volume (by quantity) even at historically ceremonial-focused houses, driven by the Häagen-Dazs (1996) and Starbucks (2005) matcha-food booms. It also notes there is no fixed certification for this split — Uji's Geographic Indication (GI) protection scheme for "Uji tea" has acknowledged limits distinguishing recently-surged mid/low-grade tencha (中下級品てん茶) from traditionally higher-grade tencha, i.e., **no formal regulatory grade-tier system exists even within the most prestigious production region.** Also of note: matcha production in Uji (and Japan broadly) overtook sencha production nationally in 2011, and the number of registered tea-ceremony schools/instructors roughly halved 2009→2018 (6,395 → 3,534 establishments) even as processing-grade matcha demand surged — direct evidence the drinking-ceremony market and the food-processing market are diverging in scale.

4. **Continuous price spectrum, not discrete grades:** Ikegaya et al. 1984 (entry #223) analyzed matcha at 5 *price points* (¥5000/4000/3000/2000/1000 per 40g) rather than named grades, and found compound content (N, amino acids, caffeine, theanine) scaling continuously with price — supporting the idea that Japanese matcha quality has traditionally been treated as a price/quality continuum rather than discrete named tiers, prior to the Western retail "ceremonial/premium/culinary/cooking" labeling convention.

5. **Connoisseurship-paradox angle** ("Much Ado About Matcha," entry #252, Kyoto Journal / Lauren W. Deutsch): the author explicitly argues that even trained chanoyu (tea ceremony) practitioners "cannot readily distinguish taste characteristics between comparable grades" of matcha — the ritual's value is framed as aesthetic/spiritual rather than a verified flavor hierarchy. This is a useful qualitative counterpoint for MatchaDB's grade-definitions page: it suggests skepticism is warranted not just about whether "ceremonial/culinary" are formal categories (they aren't), but about whether fine-grained grade distinctions are even reliably perceptible in blind tasting.

6. **China and Korea: production-parameter-based, not marketed-grade-based.** The Chinese ultrafine-grinding review (#247) cites a Chinese quality definition keyed to **D60 ≤18 μm** (a particle-size spec, similar in spirit to ISO's approach). A Guizhou provincial standard, DB52/T1358-2018, is referenced in one of the Hans Publishing market papers (#249) but its content wasn't retrievable in this pass. The Korean Hadong shading study (#244) frames its three matcha variants by **shading method**, not marketed grade name. None of the non-Japanese sources in this batch use "ceremonial"/"culinary" terminology either — reinforcing that this binary is a Western (probably US) retail-marketing overlay on what producers everywhere actually describe via production method and/or price.

## Production science and culture

- **Sensory evaluation protocol** (entry #215, Murakami 2022, 茶研報134 — a detailed how-to for matcha competition judging): 5 criteria — color (色沢), particle size (粒度), foaming (泡立ち), foam color (泡色), taste (滋味). Standard test: weigh 2 g matcha onto folding paper for appearance, or into a matcha bowl + 100 mL hot water, whisked 20 seconds, judged on all 5 axes. Extensive controlled vocabulary tables for describing color/particle/foam/taste defects (e.g. 赤み=reddish [poor shading/leaf damage], 青黒み=blue-black [hardened buds/coarse grinding], 白み=whitish [over-grinding], ざらつき=grittiness). This 2g/100mL protocol recurs in ISO/TR 21380 too (§4.5) — suggesting convergence on a de facto global-standard bench test.
- **Grinding heat and flavor:** stone mill grinding at ~50°C produces the characteristic matcha aroma via DMS generation; at 60°C, aroma becomes thin/flat and taste more diluted — narrow optimal thermal window (Murakami 2022, citing Maki et al. 1997 unpublished trial data).
- **Foaming mechanism** consensus across this batch (entries #212, #219, #220, #228): foam is a three-phase system (solid matcha particles + liquid + gas) rather than the usual two-phase liquid/gas foam. Both dissolved saponins (from tea seed/leaf) and fine solid particles independently contribute to foamability; particles specifically localize on the inside of the bubble film and appear to reinforce/stabilize it; foam stability depends more on lipid content than on pectin (contradicts older assumption that pectin was the main stabilizer) — clarified in #219 via a soluble-component-removed matcha control.
- **Whisking chemistry** (#228): vigorous whisking causes rapid water-temperature drop, suppressing EGCG dissolution (temperature-dependent solubility) — explains why prepared matcha is less bitter than raw composition would predict; foam does not meaningfully alter dissolved-compound concentration.
- **Storage/packaging:** aluminum-foil multilayer film is empirically the best-performing matcha/tea packaging material (1971 tea-packaging comparison study, entry #229, general sencha but foundational to matcha packaging practice too) — quality maintained if moisture stays ≤5.5% and vitamin C retention stays ≥70% over 3 months at 25°C/80% RH. Tencha specifically is very storage-stable in its "raw"/pre-grinding form (chlorophyllase stays inactive for 6+ months at low moisture, entry #232) — implying the standard trade practice of storing as tencha and stone-grinding just before sale/use is well supported by the chemistry.
- **Tea ceremony / cultural history:**
  - Powdered tea reached Japan via the Buddhist monk Eisai/Yōsai (栄西) around 1191-1211 CE via China; his "喫茶養生記" (1211) first promoted tea's medicinal value in Japan (Haraguchi 2015, entry #214).
  - Shading cultivation traces to reed-mat/rice-straw frost protection in the Uji area beginning ~16th century, which was then discovered to also dramatically improve flavor/color/aroma quality — i.e., shading for matcha began as an accidental frost-protection technique, not a deliberate quality intervention (entries #212, #214).
  - Historical matcha (pre-1300, mortar/pestle era) was coarse (median ~150 μm) and gritty/bitter; the shift to fine (~10 μm), smooth, umami-forward matcha is specifically a stone-mill-adoption artifact of some point after ~1300 (entry #238).
  - Korean tea culture history (entry #235, Kim Chon-Ho, 韓国の茶文化): Korea's own powdered-tea (말차/抹茶) tradition dates to Silla/Goryeo periods, with tea-grinding stone mills (다마/茶磨) documented in royal court use; Korea's tea culture crested during Goryeo (918-1392) alongside its famous celadon tea ware, then sharply declined under Joseon's Confucian anti-Buddhist policies and heavy tea taxation, nearly disappearing for ~500 years before a 19th-century revival led by the monk Chaui (草衣, 1786-1866). Useful cultural-history background distinct from the Japanese narrative, and relevant if MatchaDB ever covers Korean matcha/malcha products.
  - Book review of 田中秀隆「近代茶道の歴史社会学」(entry #234): traces how modern chanoyu's reverence for Sen no Rikyū (千利休) as tea ceremony's central historical figure was substantially a 20th-century (1898-1940) construction — his 350th-death-anniversary memorial in 1940 (synchronized with Japan's imperial 2600th-anniversary propaganda) was the key moment his cult status solidified, per the reviewed book's argument. Useful for any MatchaDB cultural/historical content, as a caution against treating "ancient tea ceremony tradition" claims uncritically.
  - "Strolling to Nothingness" (entry #253, Journal of Philosophy of Education): philosophical (not historical) analysis arguing the tea garden path (roji) functions as a universal Bildung/self-transformation pedagogy, comparable to Humboldt's Bildungsreise concept — general cultural-philosophy background, not matcha-compound-relevant.
  - Uji-region industry history (entry #236): tencha production overtook sencha production in Uji/Japan in 2011; matcha food-product demand driven initially by Häagen-Dazs matcha ice cream (1996, sourced from Kyoto tea merchants) and Starbucks matcha products (2005); Japan green tea exports up substantially, with powder-form product (matcha/powdered tea) now the majority format for US (83%) and EU/UK (68%) export destinations, vs. leaf tea.
